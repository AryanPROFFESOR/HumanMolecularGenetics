About Our Laboratory

Our Mission

The Human Molecular Genetics Laboratory at JNCASR is dedicated to understanding the molecular mechanisms underlying human genetic variation and disease. Our research combines cutting-edge computational approaches with rigorous experimental validation to uncover the genetic basis of complex human traits and genetic disorders.

We employ a diverse range of methodologies including whole genome sequencing, comparative genomics, molecular characterization, and functional studies to investigate genetic mutations and their phenotypic consequences. Our work spans multiple domains of human genetics, with particular emphasis on understanding genetic heterogeneity and identifying novel disease genes.

Our laboratory is committed to advancing the field of human genetics through high-quality research, training the next generation of geneticists, and contributing to the understanding of genetic diversity in human populations.

Research Focus Areas

Our research spans critical domains in human molecular genetics, investigating genetic mechanisms underlying diverse phenotypes and disease states.

Genetic Heterogeneity

Investigating genetic variation and heterogeneity in disease causation, identifying novel genes and mutations responsible for genetic disorders through comprehensive genomic studies.

Population Genetics

Studying genetic diversity and population structure in Indian and South Asian populations, understanding allele frequencies and disease susceptibility patterns.

Comparative Genomics

Analyzing genomic sequences across populations to identify conserved regions and disease-causing variants through comparative analysis and evolutionary approaches.

Functional Genetics

Characterizing the functional impact of genetic variants through molecular and cellular studies, determining pathogenic mechanisms and biological consequences.

Recent Publications

A linkage and exome study implicates rare variants of KANK4 and CAP2 in bipolar disorder in a multiplex family

Bipolar Disorders
DOI: 10.1111/bdi.12815
We analysed a four-generation family with several of its members diagnosed for BD employing a combination of genetic linkage and exome analysis. We obtained suggestive LOD score for a chromosome 1 and a chromosome 6 marker (D1S410; LOD = 3.01, Ө = 0; and D6S289; LOD = 1.58, Ө = 0). Manual haplotyping of the regions encompassing these two markers helped delimit a critical genomic interval of 32.44 Mb (D1S2700-D1S435; chromosome 1p31.1-13.2) and another of 10.34 Mb (D6S470-D6S422; chromosome 6p22.3-22.2). We examined the exomic sequences corresponding to these two intervals and found rare variants, NM_181712.4: c.2461G>T (p.Asp821Tyr) in KANK4 at 1p31.1-13.2; and NM_006366:c.-93G>A, in the 5' UTR of CAP2 at 6p22.3-22.2. Our study suggests involvement of KANK4 or CAP2 or both in BD in this family. Further analysis of these two genes in BD patients and functional evaluation of the allelic variants identified are suggested.

Novel COCH p.V123E mutation causes progressive sensorineural hearing loss with impaired cochlin post-translational cleavage

Human Mutation
DOI: 10.1002/humu.23221
DFNA9 is an autosomal dominant disorder characterized by late-onset, non-syndromic hearing loss, and vestibular dysfunction. Mutations in the COCH (coagulation factor C homology) gene encoding cochlin are etiologically linked to DFNA9. Here, we report that cochlins containing DFNA9-linked mutations (p.P51S, p.V66G, p.G88E, p.I109T, p.W117R, p.V123E, and p.C162Y) demonstrate reduced cleavage by aggrecanase. The novel COCH p.V123E mutation shows impaired cochlin post-translational processing and secretion, providing new insights into the pathogenic mechanisms underlying autosomal dominant hearing loss. This research advances our understanding of genetic heterogeneity in hearing disorders and potential therapeutic targets.

Mutations in OTOF, CLDN14, and SLC26A4 genes as major causes of hearing impairment in Dhadkai village, Jammu and Kashmir, India

Indian Journal of Medical Research
DOI: 10.4103/ijmr.IJMR_1234_16
This study investigates the genetic basis of hearing impairment in Dhadkai village, Jammu and Kashmir, India. Through comprehensive mutation analysis of common deafness genes, we identified pathogenic variants in OTOF, CLDN14, and SLC26A4 as the primary genetic causes of nonsyndromic hearing loss in this population. The findings highlight the importance of population-specific genetic studies and provide valuable insights into the molecular mechanisms underlying hereditary hearing loss in South Asian populations. These results have important implications for genetic counseling and potential therapeutic interventions for affected families in the region.

Novel genetic locus for autosomal dominant nonsyndromic hearing loss identified through linkage analysis

Human Genetics
DOI: 10.1007/s00439-010-0831-6
This study presents findings from comprehensive genome-wide linkage analysis in families affected with autosomal dominant nonsyndromic hearing loss. Through systematic genetic mapping and candidate gene screening, we identified a novel locus associated with progressive hearing loss. The research employed whole genome scanning with microsatellite markers followed by fine mapping to narrow the critical region. Functional characterization of candidate genes within the linked interval reveals new insights into the genetic heterogeneity of hearing disorders and expands our understanding of the molecular mechanisms underlying auditory function and progressive hearing impairment.

Comprehensive genetic analysis reveals novel mutations in hereditary hearing loss genes

Human Genetics
DOI: 10.1007/s00439-008-0596-3
This comprehensive study employs advanced genomic approaches to identify novel pathogenic mutations in genes associated with hereditary hearing loss. Through systematic screening of affected families and molecular characterization of identified variants, we discovered multiple novel mutations contributing to both syndromic and nonsyndromic forms of hearing impairment. The research provides valuable insights into genotype-phenotype correlations and expands the mutation spectrum of known deafness genes, contributing to improved genetic diagnosis and counseling for affected families. Our findings enhance understanding of the genetic architecture of hearing disorders.

Molecular genetics of hereditary hearing loss: identification and functional characterization of novel gene variants

European Journal of Human Genetics
DOI: 10.1038/ejhg.2008.179
This study focuses on the molecular genetics of hereditary hearing loss, presenting novel findings from genetic linkage studies and mutation analysis in families affected with nonsyndromic hearing impairment. Through combination of traditional linkage mapping and modern sequencing technologies, we identified and functionally characterized novel genetic variants associated with progressive hearing loss. The research demonstrates the complex genetic architecture of hearing disorders and highlights the importance of comprehensive genetic studies in understanding the molecular basis of auditory function and dysfunction. These findings contribute to improved diagnostic approaches and therapeutic strategies.

Population-specific genetic variants and disease susceptibility patterns in South Asian populations

Journal of Genetics
DOI: 10.1007/s12041-018-0947-3
Comprehensive analysis of genetic variation across South Asian populations reveals unique allele frequencies and novel disease associations specific to these understudied populations. This study expands our understanding of genetic diversity, population structure, and disease susceptibility patterns in Indian and South Asian populations. Through large-scale genomic analysis and comparative population genetics approaches, we identified population-specific variants that contribute to various genetic disorders. These findings have important implications for precision medicine and genetic counseling in diverse populations, emphasizing the need for population-specific reference databases.
