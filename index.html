About Our Laboratory

Our Mission

The Human Molecular Genetics Laboratory at JNCASR is dedicated to understanding the molecular mechanisms underlying human genetic variation and disease. Our research combines cutting-edge computational approaches with rigorous experimental validation to uncover the genetic basis of complex human traits and genetic disorders.

We employ a diverse range of methodologies including whole genome sequencing, comparative genomics, molecular characterization, and functional studies to investigate genetic mutations and their phenotypic consequences. Our work spans multiple domains of human genetics, with particular emphasis on understanding genetic heterogeneity and identifying novel disease genes.

Our laboratory is committed to advancing the field of human genetics through high-quality research, training the next generation of geneticists, and contributing to the understanding of genetic diversity in human populations.

Research Focus Areas

Our research spans critical domains in human molecular genetics, investigating genetic mechanisms underlying diverse phenotypes and disease states.

RNA Surveillance Mechanisms

Exploring the Role of RNA-Binding Proteins

Role of RNA-Binding Proteins in Stress Granule Formation and Function. Cells adapt to environmental and physiological stress by reorganizing their molecular architecture, forming dynamic RNA-protein assemblies that protect essential processes such as RNA regulation and protein synthesis. Among these assemblies, stress granules serve as key hubs for mRNA triage, preserving transcript stability and modulating translation during stress.

We study the molecular principles that shape stress granule behavior—how they assemble, disassemble, and adapt under stress. A key focus is understanding whether and how variations in regulatory proteins alter granule size, number, or dynamics, and what these changes mean for RNA regulation and cellular function.

Using advanced imaging, protein interaction mapping, and functional assays, we seek to decode the molecular framework that underpins RNA surveillance and translational control within stress granules. These insights are key to understanding fundamental cell biology and revealing how failures in RNA regulatory systems contribute to neurological disorders and other complex diseases.

Cell Cycle & Ciliopathies

Cell Cycle Regulation

We are interested in understanding the mechanisms employed by proteins involved in cell cycle regulation, ciliogenesis, apoptosis and their links to pathophysiology of various neurological disorders with a focus on genetic generalized epilepsy. Genetic analysis on cohorts of epileptic and control individuals from the Indian population has yielded a set of clinically relevant mutations which are being assayed through various cellular and animal models for their effects on cell cycle and its links to the brain.

RNA Binding Factors in Cilia Biology and Ciliopathies

Cilia are highly specialized organelles essential for numerous cellular functions, including signal transduction, fluid movement, and embryonic development. Defects in the formation or function of cilia lead to a group of disorders collectively known as ciliopathies, often characterized by developmental anomalies affecting multiple organ systems. Recent research has highlighted the emerging role of RNA-binding factors in regulating cilia biogenesis and function.

These proteins are crucial for precise RNA processing, particularly alternative splicing of genes essential for ciliogenesis and ciliary signaling. Misregulation or mutations in such factors can disrupt the splicing of ciliary genes, leading to abnormal cilia structure or signaling defects, and ultimately contribute to the pathogenesis of ciliopathies.

Understanding the molecular interplay between RNA processing machinery and ciliary pathways offers new insights into the etiology of these complex disorders and opens avenues for potential therapeutic strategies.

Protein Trafficking & Autophagy

Protein Trafficking & Autophagy

Our lab focuses on understanding how disruptions in protein trafficking contribute to epilepsy and other neurological disorders. We study proteins that regulate the movement and localization of key neuronal components, such as ion channels and receptors, which are essential for maintaining normal brain activity. Defective trafficking of these proteins can lead to imbalances in neuronal signaling and increase the risk of seizure activity.

By identifying and characterizing trafficking-related proteins implicated in epilepsy, we aim to uncover the molecular mechanisms driving the disease. Our approach combines genetic models, molecular biology, and advanced imaging to explore how these pathways are altered in the epileptic brain. This research could pave the way for targeted therapies that restore proper protein localization and neural function.

Splicing & Chromatin Biology

Investigating Chromatin Association and Transcriptional Regulatory Functions of Novel Factors

This research area focuses on understanding whether specific proteins or genes of interest play a role in chromatin biology or transcriptional regulation. A central objective is to determine whether these factors function as transcription factors, chromatin-associated proteins, or modulators of gene expression through interactions with chromatin architecture.

By using Chromatin Immunoprecipitation followed by sequencing (ChIP-seq), genome-wide binding profiles are generated to determine whether these factors associate with chromatin, bind regulatory elements such as promoters or enhancers, or function as transcription factors. The binding data are integrated with transcriptomic and epigenomic information to assess correlations with gene expression and chromatin states.

This approach helps identify whether the factors act directly on DNA or modulate chromatin structure indirectly. The ultimate goal is to reveal novel mechanisms of gene regulation and chromatin dynamics with potential implications in development and disease.

RNA Splicing Mechanisms and Gene Regulation

Alternative splicing is a fundamental mechanism that enables cells to generate protein diversity from a limited number of genes, playing critical roles in development, cellular differentiation, and disease. Our research investigates the molecular mechanisms governing splice site recognition, spliceosome assembly, and the regulatory networks that control tissue-specific and context-dependent splicing patterns.

We employ genome-wide approaches including RNA-seq and CLIP-seq to map splicing landscapes and identify regulatory elements that influence splice site selection. By integrating computational predictions with experimental validation, we characterize splicing factors and their target transcripts, revealing how aberrant splicing contributes to human disease, particularly neurological disorders and cancer.

Understanding the interplay between splicing machinery, chromatin modifications, and transcriptional regulation provides insights into how cells coordinate gene expression programs. Our work aims to identify novel splicing regulators and therapeutic targets for diseases caused by splicing defects.

Recent Publications

A linkage and exome study implicates rare variants of KANK4 and CAP2 in bipolar disorder in a multiplex family

Bipolar Disorders
DOI: 10.1111/bdi.12815
DFNA9 is an autosomal dominant disorder characterized by late-onset, non-syndromic hearing loss, and vestibular dysfunction. Mutations in the COCH (coagulation factor C homology) gene encoding cochlin are etiologically linked to DFNA9. Here, we report that cochlins containing DFNA9-linked mutations (p.P51S, p.V66G, p.G88E, p.I109T, p.W117R, p.V123E, and p.C162Y) demonstrate reduced cleavage by aggrecanase. The novel COCH p.V123E mutation shows impaired cochlin post-translational processing and secretion, providing new insights into the pathogenic mechanisms underlying autosomal dominant hearing loss.

We analysed a four-generation family with several of its members diagnosed for BD employing a combination of genetic linkage and exome analysis. We obtained suggestive LOD score for a chromosome 1 and a chromosome 6 marker (D1S410; LOD = 3.01, θ = 0; and D6S289; LOD = 1.58, θ = 0). Manual haplotyping of the regions encompassing these two markers helped delimit a critical genomic interval of 32.44 Mb (D1S2700-D1S435; chromosome 1p31.1-13.2) and another of 10.34 Mb (D6S470-D6S422; chromosome 6p22.3-22.2). We examined the exomic sequences corresponding to these two intervals and found rare variants, NM_181712.4: c.2461G>T (p.Asp821Tyr) in KANK4 at 1p31.1-13.2; and NM_006366:c.-93G>A, in the 5' UTR of CAP2 at 6p22.3-22.2. Our study suggests involvement of KANK4 or CAP2 or both in BD in this family. Further analysis of these two genes in BD patients and functional evaluation of the allelic variants identified are suggested.
