A linkage and exome study implicates rare variants of KANK4 and CAP2 in bipolar disorder in a multiplex family

Bipolar Disorders
DOI: 10.1111/bdi.12815
We analysed a four-generation family with several of its members diagnosed for BD employing a combination of genetic linkage and exome analysis. We obtained suggestive LOD score for a chromosome 1 and a chromosome 6 marker (D1S410; LOD = 3.01, θ = 0; and D6S289; LOD = 1.58, θ = 0). Manual haplotyping of the regions encompassing these two markers helped delimit a critical genomic interval of 32.44 Mb (D1S2700-D1S435; chromosome 1p31.1-13.2) and another of 10.34 Mb (D6S470-D6S422; chromosome 6p22.3-22.2). We examined the exomic sequences corresponding to these two intervals and found rare variants, NM_181712.4: c.2461G>T (p.Asp821Tyr) in KANK4 at 1p31.1-13.2; and NM_006366:c.-93G>A, in the 5' UTR of CAP2 at 6p22.3-22.2. Our study suggests involvement of KANK4 or CAP2 or both in BD in this family. Further analysis of these two genes in BD patients and functional evaluation of the allelic variants identified are suggested.

Novel COCH p.V123E mutation causes progressive sensorineural hearing loss with impaired cochlin post-translational cleavage

Human Mutation
DOI: 10.1002/humu.23221
DFNA9 is an autosomal dominant disorder characterized by late-onset, non-syndromic hearing loss, and vestibular dysfunction. Mutations in the COCH (coagulation factor C homology) gene encoding cochlin are etiologically linked to DFNA9. Here, we report that cochlins containing DFNA9-linked mutations (p.P51S, p.V66G, p.G88E, p.I109T, p.W117R, p.V123E, and p.C162Y) demonstrate reduced cleavage by aggrecanase. The novel COCH p.V123E mutation shows impaired cochlin post-translational processing and secretion, providing new insights into the pathogenic mechanisms underlying autosomal dominant hearing loss.

Mutations in OTOF, CLDN14, and SLC26A4 genes as major causes of hearing impairment in Dhadkai village, Jammu and Kashmir, India

Indian Journal of Medical Research
DOI: 10.4103/ijmr.IJMR_1234_16
This study investigates the genetic basis of hearing impairment in Dhadkai village, Jammu and Kashmir, India. Through comprehensive mutation analysis of common deafness genes, we identified pathogenic variants in OTOF, CLDN14, and SLC26A4 as the primary genetic causes of nonsyndromic hearing loss in this population. The findings highlight the importance of population-specific genetic studies and provide valuable insights into the molecular mechanisms underlying hereditary hearing loss in South Asian populations.

Novel genetic locus for autosomal dominant nonsyndromic hearing loss identified through linkage analysis

Human Genetics
DOI: 10.1007/s00439-010-0831-6
This study presents findings from comprehensive genome-wide linkage analysis in families affected with autosomal dominant nonsyndromic hearing loss. Through systematic genetic mapping and candidate gene screening, we identified a novel locus associated with progressive hearing loss. The research employed whole genome scanning with microsatellite markers followed by fine mapping to narrow the critical region.

DOI: 10.1007/s00439-008-0596-3
This comprehensive study employs advanced genomic approaches to identify novel pathogenic mutations in genes associated with hereditary hearing loss. European Journal of Human Genetics
DOI: 10.1038/ejhg.2008.179
This study focuses on the molecular genetics of hereditary hearing loss, presenting novel findings from genetic linkage studies and mutation analysis in families affected with nonsyndromic hearing impairment. Through combination of traditional linkage mapping and modern sequencing technologies, we identified and functionally characterized novel genetic variants associated with progressive hearing loss.
